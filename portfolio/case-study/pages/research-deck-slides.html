Overview / Research deck slides

Slide-ready content — Netnographic research & SME positioning

Use for: Google Slides, Keynote, PDF deck, or Loom chapter markers
Audience: Hiring manager / design lead
Length: 12 slides (standalone research deck) or slides 3b–3h inserted into 12-presentation-deck-outline.md
Sources: 17-netnographic-ats-research.md · 18-market-research-summary.md

Slide 1 — Title

Designing an ATS for teams that can’t afford to mis-buy

Secondary netnography · TalentHub · May 2026

On slide

  • Your name · Product Designer
  • Subtitle: Research → SME product intent → Applications design

Visual: Split — small team at laptop / enterprise pricing table blurred in background

Speaker note (30s): This deck shows how public discourse about ATS tools shaped a product aimed at small and medium organizations — not enterprise procurement.

Slide 2 — Product intent

TalentHub is built for SME / SMB hiring teams

On slide (3 columns)

Low cost Good UX Fast setup
Core loop only — no CRM bloat Resume in 1–2 actions Days/weeks, not 6+ week implementations
No $4K–$25K enterprise stack Honest pipeline state Sensible defaults, lean admin
Predictable scope No “back to Excel” days Local-first, focused backoffice

Visual: Three icon cards; target persona: 20–200 employees, 1–3 recruiters

Speaker note: SMEs need credible hiring workflow without paying for modules they’ll never open.

Slide 3 — Why research first

Vendor sites sell features. Users sell warnings.

On slide

  • ATS = essential but resented (recruiters + candidates)
  • Only 43% of HR pros rate their TA stack “good or excellent”
  • SMEs face a squeeze: enterprise = too costly · budget tools = too shallow

Visual: Hero stat 43% with footnote HR.com 2025–26

Speaker note: Before IA or pixels, I needed the vocabulary of frustration — especially what lean teams say when their first ATS purchase goes wrong.

Slide 4 — Method

Secondary netnography — public discourse synthesis

On slide (4 tiles)

  1. Reviews — G2, TrustRadius · renewal intent, head-to-head
  2. Recruiter blogs — daily workflow pain
  3. HR / talent press — trust, compliance incidents
  4. Candidate surveys — abandonment, ghosting, opacity

Footer: Directional for strategy · not statistically representative

Visual: Simple source-type icons in a row

Slide 5 — The SME gap

Two bad options in the market

On slide (2×2)

Too much Too little
Enterprise (Greenhouse, Lever, Workday) Cost · setup · admin overhead —
Budget SMB (JazzHR, Workable) — Shallow workflow · outgrow quickly

Pull quote (center): Mid-market craft at SMB scope

Visual: Positioning map — legacy/clunky ↔ modern UX; enterprise ↔ startup; TalentHub dot in “modern + lean” quadrant

Speaker note: Ashby gets close for startups, but discourse still skews Series A–C. The gap is the 50-person company with one recruiter.

Slide 6 — Two voices

Recruiters measure clicks. Candidates measure silence.

On slide (split)

Recruiters ask… Candidates ask…
“How many clicks to the resume?” “Did anyone read this?”
“Why am I back in Excel?” “Why no update after apply?”
“Why does status lie?” “Was a human involved?”

Visual: Large contrasting quotes, two columns

Slide 7 — Recruiter pain (ranked)

What discourse repeats most

On slide (max 5 bullets)

  1. Too many clicks — resume not focal
  2. Weak search — keywords over fit
  3. Shallow automation — bulk reject yes; scheduling still manual
  4. Pipeline noise — all-time boards; status ≠ reality
  5. Reporting / audit gaps — exports need workarounds

SME callout: No TA ops admin — UX failure → Excel shadow ATS

Visual: Horizontal priority bars or numbered list

Slide 8 — Candidate pain (the numbers)

Opacity erodes trust

On slide (2×2 stat grid)

~60% ~57–60%
Exasperated by not knowing if a human reviewed their resume Abandon when forms are long/complex
~65% ~41%
Rarely/never get updates after applying Believe 0–25% of apps are seen by a recruiter

Visual: One stat per quadrant; “Ghosting” as accent label

Slide 9 — What SMEs actually buy

Buyer discourse = economics + adoption

On slide

  • Predictable TCO — no surprise fees
  • Fast go-live — not a quarter-long project
  • Recruiters and hiring managers use it
  • Core loop only — apply → triage → interview → hire/reject

Visual: Checklist with “SME pass/fail” framing

Speaker note: Tracker RMS and buyer guides treat UX adoption and honest TCO as pass/fail — SMEs fail permanently when those fail.

Slide 10 — Research → design

How findings shaped TalentHub Applications

On slide (two columns, 6 rows max)

Market pain Design response
Enterprise cost / bloat Core loop scope — no CRM marketplace
Infinite pipeline Week-scoped cohort toolbar
Scattered review Single packet page /applications/[id]
Scan vs funnel Table + pipeline tabs
Dishonest status 10 statuses, validated transitions, audit
Status vs interview mismatch Schedule interview gate
Lost context ?from=applications back path
AI without trust Advisory scores + human-review notice

Visual: Product screenshot annotated with 3 callouts (pipeline week, packet page, status gate)

Speaker note: I didn’t copy Greenhouse — I addressed the same frustrations their users still post about, at a scope a 50-person company could adopt.

Slide 11 — Honest scope

What we built vs what we deferred

On slide (split bar)

Built for SME triage

  • Applications hub · governed Kanban · packet review · scheduling · document access

Deferred (enterprise / later)

  • CRM nurture · deep analytics · candidate email · integration marketplace · full RBAC

Pull quote: Better workflow integrity than budget tools — without enterprise packaging.

Visual: “In scope” green / “Later” neutral tags

Slide 12 — Close

Research informed the spec — and the business model

On slide

  • Full report: netnographic synthesis (May 2026)
  • Next research: 5–8 moderated recruiter interviews; candidate think-aloud on apply
  • CTA: Case study · Figma · Contact

Sources (small type): NUROFILE · TrustRadius · HR Dive · LiveCareer · Tracker RMS · HR.com

Transition one-liners

Design tips

  • One hero stat per slide — don’t crowd (43%, 60%, 65%)
  • Slide 2 + 5 establish SME intent early — don’t bury the “why this product exists”
  • Slide 10 is the payoff — screenshot beats another table
  • Slide 11 shows maturity — deferring enterprise scope is a feature for SME positioning
  • Keep competitor names to 4 rows on stage; full table in appendix
